What is tiger's eye?

Tiger's eye is a quartz pseudomorph — a stone formed when one mineral gradually replaces another while preserving the original structure. It begins as crocidolite, a fibrous blue amphibole mineral (a variety of asbestos), which is slowly replaced by silica over millions of years through a geological process called silicification. As the iron in the original crocidolite oxidises during this replacement, it produces iron oxide, which colours the resulting stone golden yellow to rich brown. The fibrous structure of the original crocidolite is preserved within the quartz, and it is this parallel fibre arrangement that produces tiger's eye's most celebrated characteristic.

That characteristic is chatoyancy — from the French chatoyer, meaning "to shine like a cat's eye." As a polished tiger's eye bead is rotated, a single band of bright silky light appears to glide across its surface, mimicking the reflective slit pupil of a feline eye. This optical effect only occurs in stones cut perpendicular to the direction of the fibres. At Mohs 7, tiger's eye is durable enough for everyday wear as a bracelet. The primary commercial source is South Africa, with significant production also from India and Western Australia.

Traditional associations

Tiger's eye holds one of the most well-documented histories of cultural use among semi-precious stones. Ancient Roman soldiers are recorded in historical texts to have carried tiger's eye into battle, regarding it as a stone associated with courage, sharp perception, and strength. The visual quality of the stone — the moving light band that seems to see in all directions — made it a natural symbol of watchful awareness across several military cultures of antiquity.

In ancient Egypt, tiger's eye was used in the carved eyes of deity statues, chosen for the lifelike quality its chatoyancy gave to sculptured figures. The stone appears in the ornamentation of several significant archaeological finds from the New Kingdom period. Its golden tones also aligned with Egyptian associations between gold and the divine, making tiger's eye one of the more symbolically layered stones in ancient Egyptian material culture.

Across various Asian traditions, tiger's eye has historically been associated with clear-eyed thinking and decision-making — the visual metaphor of a stone that appears to "see" from every angle mapping naturally onto concepts of discernment and perceptiveness in several philosophical and cultural contexts. In traditional South Asian lapidary practice, tiger's eye was grouped among stones associated with courage and mental clarity, though the specific application of these associations varied by regional and historical context. These cultural associations are documented as historical practices, not scientific claims about the stone's effects.

Wearing it as a bracelet

Tiger's eye is one of the most visually dynamic semi-precious stones available in bracelet form — the chatoyancy means the bracelet genuinely changes appearance as the wrist moves through light. A strand of well-cut 8mm tiger's eye beads will show the silky light band rippling across the beads in sequence as the arm moves, which no other common affordable semi-precious stone can replicate. The colour is warm and earthy: golden amber, honey, and deep brown in distinct bands across each bead. It suits warm skin tones particularly well and pairs naturally with yellow gold hardware. For stacking, it combines well with black tourmaline (strong tonal contrast) or with citrine (warm golden harmony). Men's and gender-neutral styling has made tiger's eye particularly popular in that demographic, though it suits any wrist.

How to spot real tiger's eye

Tiger's eye is one of the easier semi-precious stones to verify, because the chatoyancy effect is very difficult to fake convincingly at low cost. However, dyed tiger's eye (sold as "blue tiger's eye" or "red tiger's eye") and glass cat's eye (fibre-optic glass) are common in the market and should be understood before buying.

Test 1 — Chatoyancy quality

Move the bead under a direct light source (phone torch or natural sunlight). Genuine tiger's eye shows a single, clearly defined silky band of light that moves smoothly across the surface as you rotate the bead. The band should look organic and slightly imperfect in definition. Fibre-optic glass imitations show a very sharp, unnaturally perfect band and often have a more vivid uniform colour.

Test 2 — Colour banding

Natural tiger's eye has distinct parallel banding in alternating lighter and darker golden-brown tones, caused by the original fibrous mineral structure. Dyed imitations and glass often lack this natural banding and appear more uniformly coloured. Look for organic colour variation within each bead.

Test 3 — "Blue" and "red" tiger's eye

Genuine hawk's eye (blue tiger's eye) is a real natural stone — it is tiger's eye where oxidation was incomplete, leaving the crocidolite partially intact. Red tiger's eye, however, is almost always heat-treated or dyed natural tiger's eye; naturally red specimens are rare. If buying coloured variants, ask the seller whether the colour is natural or treated.

Test 4 — Weight and temperature

As a quartz-based stone at Mohs 7, tiger's eye feels cool and is denser than most glass imitations of equivalent size. Glass fibre-optic cat's eye (a common substitute) is slightly lighter and warms faster in the hand.

Reliable listings for tiger's eye specify bead size, country of origin (South Africa being the most reputable source for natural colour stones), and whether the colour has been enhanced by heat treatment or dyeing. Sellers who are vague about these details are worth approaching with caution.

Care guide

Water

Safe for brief water contact. Mohs 7 makes it resilient for everyday wear. Avoid prolonged soaking and harsh detergents which can dull the surface polish and affect the appearance of the chatoyancy.

Sunlight

Tiger's eye is sun-stable — the golden-brown iron oxide colour is not susceptible to UV fading under normal wear conditions. Avoid extreme prolonged heat, which can affect any stone's surface over time.

Cleaning

Wipe with a slightly damp soft cloth. The chatoyancy effect comes from the polished surface — avoid abrasive cloths or chemical polishes that could micro-scratch the finish and reduce the silky light effect.

Storage

Store in a soft pouch. Tiger's eye at Mohs 7 can scratch softer stones. The surface polish is what creates the chatoyancy — protect it from abrasion in storage by keeping it separate from other jewellery.

Frequently asked questions

What is the chatoyancy effect in tiger's eye?

Chatoyancy is an optical phenomenon where a moving band of bright silky light appears to glide across the surface of a stone as it is rotated. In tiger's eye, it is caused by parallel fibres of crocidolite that have been replaced by silica over millions of years, preserving the fibrous structure in quartz. When light hits these parallel fibres, it reflects in a single concentrated bright band — the same optical principle as a cat's eye.

Is tiger's eye safe to wear?

Yes. Tiger's eye is completely safe to wear as jewellery. Although it forms through the replacement of crocidolite (a type of asbestos), the finished polished stone contains no free asbestos fibres — the fibres are fully encased within quartz and cannot be inhaled or contacted. Polished tiger's eye beads present no known health risk.

What is the difference between tiger's eye, hawk's eye, and cat's eye?

Tiger's eye is the golden-brown variety formed when crocidolite is fully oxidised during silicification. Hawk's eye (also called falcon's eye) is the blue-grey variety of the same mineral, where oxidation was incomplete and the crocidolite remains partially unoxidised, preserving the blue colour. Cat's eye is a general term for any chatoyant stone, but most specifically refers to cymophane (chrysoberyl cat's eye), which is a distinct and different mineral.

Can tiger's eye get wet?

Brief water contact is safe — tiger's eye is a quartz pseudomorph with a Mohs hardness of 7 and handles everyday moisture without damage. Avoid prolonged soaking and harsh chemical cleaning agents, which can dull the polished surface finish that produces the chatoyancy effect.

Is tiger's eye a naturally occurring stone?

Yes. Tiger's eye is a naturally occurring mineral formed through silicification of crocidolite fibres over millions of years. The golden-brown colour is produced by iron oxide that forms when the iron-containing crocidolite oxidises during this geological replacement process. It is mined primarily in South Africa, India, and Western Australia.
